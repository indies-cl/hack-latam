# deck copywriting critique

_through the lens of naval ravikant & paul graham_

---

## the good

the deck has bones. it's short, direct, lowercase, and doesn't hide behind corporate fluff. that alone puts it ahead of 90% of sponsorship decks. the "deployed software only" line is genuinely strong — it's a filter that signals taste.

but "good enough" is the enemy of great. let's tear it apart.

---

## 1. you're selling features, not leverage

> **naval:** "give me a lever long enough and a place to stand, and I will move the world."

the "why sponsor" section reads like a SaaS feature list:

> - talent access: direct line to 150 vetted builders
> - product usage: your tools battle-tested by real teams
> - brand positioning: visible support for execution-focused talent

this is a **menu**, not a **narrative**. you're listing what sponsors *get* but never articulating the **asymmetric upside**. naval would ask: where's the leverage? a sponsor isn't buying logo placement — they're buying a **lottery ticket on 30+ teams** who might become the next rappi, kavak, or nubank.

**rewrite instinct:** don't sell the event. sell the **optionality** the event creates. "you get early access to 30+ teams building solutions to latam's hardest problems — before anyone else knows they exist."

---

## 2. "let's code for a better world" — says nothing

> **pg:** "the way to get a big idea to appear in your head is not to hunt for big ideas, but to put in a lot of time on work that interests you."

"let's code for a better world" is the hackathon equivalent of "we're making the world a better place." it's a silicon valley cliché that paul graham has explicitly mocked. it sounds like a tagline written by committee.

the spanish version — "programemos un mundo mejor" — has the same problem.

**the fix:** either be *dangerously specific* or be *provocatively honest*. something like: "54 hours. 150 builders. four problems latam can't keep ignoring." — that's a tagline with teeth.

---

## 3. you buried the insight

> **pg:** "the most important thing is to be able to think of new ideas. not the kind that are merely new, but the kind that are true."

the single most compelling line in the entire deck is buried in the middle of a bullet list:

> "most hackathons optimize for photos, not outcomes."

that's a **thesis**. that should be front and center — the *entire* positioning of the event should flow from this observation. instead, you treated it like seasoning when it's the main course.

naval would frame it as: "the market for hackathons is broken. they optimize for vanity, not value. we're the correction."

---

## 4. "hand-picked" and "vetted" — prove it or drop it

> **naval:** "specific knowledge is knowledge you cannot be trained for. if society can train you, it can train someone else and replace you."

you use "hand-picked" and "vetted" multiple times but never explain the filter. what's your acceptance rate? what's the application process? what signal are you selecting on?

paul graham would say: **"show, don't tell."** anyone can say "vetted." only someone with a real process can say "last event: 400 applications, 40 accepted. average applicant had 2+ shipped products."

numbers are credibility. adjectives are not.

---

## 5. the ask is timid

> **naval:** "if you're not willing to bet on yourself, why should anyone else?"

"$5k usd total" with "(contributions of any size welcome)" reads like you're apologizing for asking. this is a tell. it signals you're not confident in the value you're offering.

paul graham's advice to founders: never negotiate against yourself. if the event is as good as you claim — 150 vetted builders, real deployable products, social impact tracks — then $5k is either too low or you haven't justified why it's the right number.

**either:** raise the ask and justify it with economics ("$5k funds prizes that incentivize 150 engineers to build on your platform for 54 hours — that's $0.60/engineer-hour of product exposure"), **or:** remove the number entirely and make it a conversation ("let's talk about what makes sense for your goals").

---

## 6. you're playing a status game when you should play a wealth game

> **naval:** "play iterated games. all the returns in life come from compound interest."

the deck is structured as a one-time transaction: you give us money, we give you logos and stage time. but the **real value** is the compounding relationship. if a team from hack@latam goes on to raise $10M and they're using your infra — that's a 1000x return on a $1k sponsorship.

you never mention follow-up. you never mention the alumni network. you never mention what happened to teams from previous events. **the compounding story is absent.**

pg would say: "do things that don't scale" — but *also* tell people about the things that will scale later.

---

## 7. "four fault lines in latam" — great. then what?

> **pg:** "an essay should be surprising."

"four fault lines in latam. each track is about data, coordination, and real-time action." — this is the strongest structural insight in the deck. but the track descriptions that follow are generic:

> "use data to expose, track, and reduce corruption"
> "software for organizing, helping, and acting together"

these could be written by chatgpt for any hackathon in any region. where's the **latam-specific** angle? what makes corruption in latam different from corruption anywhere else? what specific data sources exist? what coordination failures are unique to the region?

naval's filter: **"if it's not specific knowledge, it's not valuable."** make each track description so specific that it could *only* apply to latin america.

---

## 8. "measurable, not fuzzy" — then measure something

> **pg:** "when experts are wrong, it's often because they're experts on an earlier version of the world."

you end the "what you get" section with "measurable, not fuzzy." strong closer. but you never actually provide a measurement. how many impressions did the last event generate? what's the discord engagement rate? how many people watch the streams?

the line becomes **ironic** without data backing it up. it's a promise of measurement with zero measurements.

---

## 9. the voice is inconsistent

paul graham writes in one voice — conversational, precise, slightly contrarian. your deck oscillates between:

- **hacker casual:** "people who actually ship"
- **corporate:** "visible support for execution-focused talent"
- **motivational poster:** "let's code for a better world"

pick one voice and commit. the hacker voice is your strongest — it's authentic to who you are. every time you drift into corporate-speak ("brand positioning," "recruiting pipeline"), you sound like you're wearing a costume.

---

## 10. you forgot to tell a story

> **naval:** "the most important skill for getting rich is becoming a perpetual learner."
> **pg:** "the way to write a great essay is to write the essay only you could write."

the entire deck is structured as: here's what we are, here's what we do, here's what we want. it's informational, not **transformational**.

where's the story of the team from the last hackathon that actually shipped something? where's the builder who came as a student and left as a founder? where's the moment that made you realize this needed to exist?

naval would say: "you're selling a product when you should be selling a **belief**."

pg would say: "write the essay that only you could write." right now, 50 hackathon organizers in latam could have written this deck. what's the version that only **you** could write?

---

## tl;dr

| problem | fix |
|---|---|
| tagline is generic | make it specific and provocative |
| selling features not leverage | sell the asymmetric upside / optionality |
| best line is buried | lead with "hackathons optimize for photos, not outcomes" |
| "vetted" without proof | add acceptance rates, numbers, outcomes |
| ask is timid | either justify the number or remove it |
| no compounding story | show what happens *after* the event |
| tracks are generic | make them latam-specific with real data angles |
| "measurable" with no metrics | add actual numbers |
| voice is inconsistent | commit to the hacker voice |
| no story | add one real human transformation |
